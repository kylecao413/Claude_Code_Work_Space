## 2. Likely Questions + Model Answers

### Q1. "Tell us about your firm and what plan reviews you've done before."
*Why they ask:* Baseline competency check.
> "KCY Engineer PLLC is my Virginia plan-review and engineering firm; BCC is my DC-based third-party inspection agency. On the plan review side, I've been doing third-party plan reviews for DC projects across Building, Mechanical, Electrical, Plumbing, and Fire Protection — small commercial fit-outs up through mid-rise renovations. Most of my volume to date has been DC, but the code work translates directly to VCC 2021."

### Q2. "Which disciplines do you intend to review?"
*Why they ask:* Scope your authorization.
> "For Fairfax, I'd like to be qualified on Mechanical, Electrical, Plumbing, and Fire Protection — those map to my own PE seal and my MCP. I'm also comfortable with the Building discipline through code consulting, but I'd defer to whatever scope Fairfax thinks is appropriate to start."

### Q3. "How will you handle a conflict of interest — e.g., you designed it, or you're the inspector of record?"
*Why they ask:* This is the #1 program-integrity concern.
> "Hard rule: I won't accept a plan review on any project where my firm did the design, or where I'm engaged as inspector of record. If a conflict surfaces mid-review, I disclose immediately and withdraw. I'd rather lose the fee than the credential."

### Q4. "Walk us through your plan-review process — first pass to approval."
*Why they ask:* Want to hear discipline and structure.
> "I do a code-applicability matrix first — occupancy, construction type, sprinkler status, height/area — so I'm reviewing against the right sections. Then I go discipline-by-discipline against VCC 2021 and the referenced standards. Deficiencies get logged with a code citation and a sheet/detail reference. I issue a written deficiency report, the design team responds with revisions and a comment-response letter, and I verify each item is addressed before issuing approval."

### Q5. "What's your turnaround SLA?"
*Why they ask:* Expedited means fast.
> "For a typical commercial fit-out, first-pass review in 5 to 7 business days; back-check on revisions in 2 to 3. For larger or multi-discipline jobs I'd scope the timeline up front with the applicant. Whatever Fairfax sets as the program standard, I'll meet or beat it."

### Q6. "How do you document deficiencies?"
*Why they ask:* They want consistent, defensible reports.
> "Each item: code section cited, sheet and detail reference, plain-language description of the deficiency, and what's needed to resolve it. I number them so the response letter can address each one explicitly. Happy to use whatever template Fairfax provides."

### Q7. "What's your QA process — who checks the reviewer?"
*Why they ask:* Single-PE shops are a risk.
> "On disciplines outside my primary stamp, I have collaborating PEs who peer-review before issuance. For my own primary disciplines, I do a checklist-driven self-QA — code matrix, deficiency log, response verification — and I keep a written record of each step in the project file."

### Q8. "Insurance — do you carry E&O / professional liability?"
*Why they ask:* Standard county requirement.
> "Yes — KCY carries professional liability. I'll send a current COI naming Fairfax County as a certificate holder if that's the program requirement. Just let me know the limits you need."

(Action item: confirm COI limits Fairfax expects, and have agent issue endorsement.)

### Q9. "How do you stay current on code updates?"
*Why they ask:* MCPs are expected to be students of the code.
> "ICC continuing education for the MCP renewal cycle, ICC chapter meetings, and I track VCC and IFC amendments through DHCD and the county code pages. When a new edition lands I work through the significant-changes summaries before I touch a project under it."

### Q10. "What's your relationship with the design professional during review — collaborative or adversarial?"
*Why they ask:* They want collaborative; adversarial reviewers eat staff time.
> "Collaborative. The goal is a code-compliant building, not a paper fight. I'll pick up the phone before I write a comment if something looks like a misunderstanding. The deficiency report is for genuine code issues, not preference."

### Q11. "Describe a tough plan review you've done."
*Why they ask:* Real-world judgment.
> "A mixed-use fit-out in DC — restaurant on the ground floor with a Type I hood, residential above. The grease duct routing conflicted with rated assemblies and the existing standpipe. We worked through it with the MEP and FP designers over two rounds — re-routing, adding shaft protection, and confirming hood suppression interlocks. The lesson was to flag the cross-discipline issues in the first pass so the team can solve them together, not serially."

### Q12. "Why expedited — what's your fee model?"
*Why they ask:* Fee-fishing or program-design question.
> "Honestly, expedited fits my workflow — I'd rather do fewer projects faster than sit on a queue. On fees, I assume Fairfax has a program structure; I'm happy to work within it. If fee-setting is on the reviewer, I'd want to understand the program norms first before I quote." (不主动报价)

## 3. Questions for YOU to Ask Fairfax

Asking signals you've thought about the work. 6–8 is plenty — pick what fits the flow.

1. **Submission platform** — "Are reviewers working in ePlans / Bluebeam Studio / PDF markups? Any preferred markup conventions Fairfax wants us to follow?"
2. **Deficiency report template** — "Does the county provide a standard deficiency report template, or do reviewers use their own?"
3. **Applicant communication** — "Do I correspond directly with the design professional and applicant, or does everything route through county staff?"
4. **Conflict of interest** — "Beyond the design/inspector-of-record cases, are there other conflict scenarios the program flags? (e.g., same GC across multiple jobs, prior consulting on the site)"
5. **Turnaround standards** — "What's the program's expected turnaround, and at what point does a slow reviewer get pulled from the rotation?"
6. **Re-review fees** — "How are second/third back-checks handled — flat fee, hourly, or capped?"
7. **Recertification** — "What's the renewal cadence for the third-party reviewer credential, and any continuing-education hours required?"
8. **Code interpretation contact** — "When I hit a genuinely ambiguous code section mid-review, who at the county is the right person to call for an interpretation?"

## 4. Red Flags to Avoid

- **Don't oversell.** "We can do anything" is a tell. Stick to disciplines you can actually stamp or peer-review.
- **Don't badmouth.** No comments about other reviewers, slow county staff, or "the old way." Stay positive.
- **Don't over-commit volume.** If they ask "how many can you handle a month?" — answer with a range and say it depends on size/discipline mix.
- **Don't quote fees unprompted.** Let them surface the program's fee structure first.
- **Don't acknowledge "the other person."** Treat them as a peer in the room. No side comments.
- **Don't ramble on credentials.** PE + MCP + Fairfax 3PI is enough — let them ask for more.
- **Don't pitch BCC's DC inspection work as if it's a Fairfax credential.** Different jurisdiction, different program. Mention it as background context only.
- **Avoid filler ("um," "you know")** — pause is fine; pause beats filler.
